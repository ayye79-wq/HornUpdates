The Battle for Sudan's Future: Can Civil Society Break the Military's Grip?

OpinionSudanSouth SudanEritreaEthiopia By Amira Hassan  ·  July 20, 2026
Opinion notice: This is analysis and commentary by Horn Updates editors. It does not represent the position of any government, institution, or external party.
What this means
  • Sudan's military faces rising civil resistance
  • Humanitarian crisis deepening amid conflict
  • International actors reconsidering engagement strategies

The ongoing conflict in Sudan has entered a perilous phase, marked by intensified military clashes and a deepening humanitarian crisis. As the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) vie for control, the hopes of a nascent democratic movement hang in the balance. Yet, amidst this turmoil, civil society has begun to organize, presenting a formidable challenge to the entrenched military establishment. The question remains: can these grassroots movements catalyze a genuine reform agenda that prioritizes the needs of the Sudanese people over military ambitions?

The Current Landscape: A Struggle for Control

The conflict in Sudan erupted in April 2023, following a power struggle between General Abdel Fattah al-Burhan, the leader of the SAF, and General Mohamed Hamdan Dagalo (known as Hemetti), the head of the RSF. This violent confrontation has resulted in thousands of deaths and the displacement of millions. As of mid-October 2023, the United Nations estimates that over 5 million people have been displaced due to the fighting, with more than 24 million—over half the population—requiring humanitarian assistance.

In this context, the military has not only failed to provide security but has actively contributed to the humanitarian disaster. Reports indicate that both the SAF and RSF have engaged in widespread atrocities, including targeting civilians and obstructing aid deliveries. The sense of despair among the population is palpable, fueling anger and frustration towards the military leadership and prompting calls for accountability.

The Emergence of Civil Society: A New Force?

In recent weeks, a coalition of civil society organizations has begun to mobilize, aiming to reclaim the narrative of Sudan's political future. Groups such as the Sudanese Professionals Association and various women's rights organizations are at the forefront of this movement. They advocate for a civilian-led government and emphasize the importance of inclusive dialogue that encompasses all sectors of society, including marginalized communities.

This grassroots mobilization can be traced back to the 2018-2019 protests that ultimately led to the ousting of long-time dictator Omar al-Bashir. The lessons learned from that struggle—particularly the need for unity and resilience—are informing current efforts. Activists argue that the military's violent response to dissent only strengthens their resolve, as they recognize the urgency of transitioning to a democratic governance structure.

The Role of International Actors: Competing Interests

The international community's response to the crisis in Sudan has been varied and often tepid. While some countries have called for an immediate ceasefire and a return to civilian rule, others have been more focused on maintaining strategic relationships with military leaders. The United States, for instance, has expressed support for civilian governance, yet its actions, including sanctions against certain military leaders, have been inconsistent.

Moreover, regional players such as Egypt and the United Arab Emirates have vested interests in the outcome of the Sudanese conflict, complicating the international response. For Egypt, a stable Sudan is crucial for its national security, particularly concerning the flow of the Nile River. Meanwhile, the UAE has made significant investments in Sudan's agricultural sector, making it wary of any developments that could jeopardize its economic interests.

Looking Ahead: The Potential for Reform

The potential for meaningful reform in Sudan hinges on several factors. First, civil society must sustain its momentum and continue to garner support from the populace. To do so, it will need to articulate a clear vision that addresses the grievances of various communities, including ethnic minorities and women, who have historically been sidelined in political discussions.

Second, the international community must recalibrate its approach to engagement with Sudan. Support should be directed towards empowering civil society groups rather than propping up military leaders who have demonstrated a willingness to exploit power for personal gain. A unified stance that prioritizes humanitarian aid and democratic governance could help tip the balance in favor of a civilian-led transition.

Finally, the Sudanese military must recognize that its path forward is untenable in the long run. As protests and civil disobedience intensify, it risks facing a level of unrest that could undermine its authority entirely. History has shown that military regimes that ignore the voices of their citizens ultimately pay a steep price.

In conclusion, while the challenges facing Sudan are formidable, the growing strength of civil society offers a glimmer of hope. If these organizations can unify their efforts and engage effectively with the international community, they may well succeed in steering the country towards a more democratic future. The coming months will be critical; the world will be watching to see if Sudan's battle for reform will be won or lost in the streets.
