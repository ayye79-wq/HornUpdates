The Unraveling of Sudan's Military Alliance: Implications for the Region

OpinionSudanEthiopiaSomaliaSouth SudanEritreaKenyaDjiboutiSomaliland By Amira Hassan  ·  April 27, 2026
Opinion notice: This is analysis and commentary by Horn Updates editors. It does not represent the position of any government, institution, or external party.

In recent months, Sudan has experienced a tumultuous period marked by escalating conflicts between military factions, particularly the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF). This conflict, rooted in a complex web of historical grievances and power struggles, has not only destabilized Sudan but also poses significant risks for the entire Horn of Africa. As factions fracture and the military landscape evolves, the implications for regional stability are profound.

The Power Struggle: SAF vs. RSF

The ongoing conflict in Sudan is primarily a power struggle between the SAF, led by General Abdel Fattah al-Burhan, and the RSF, commanded by General Mohamed Hamdan Dagalo, known as Hemedti. This rivalry intensified following the 2021 coup, which saw both generals initially collaborating to oust the civilian-led government. However, their alliance has since deteriorated into open conflict, characterized by brutal fighting that has displaced millions and exacerbated humanitarian crises.

Recent reports indicate that the SAF has begun to consolidate control over key urban areas, while the RSF has adopted guerrilla tactics, leveraging its mobile units to maintain influence in rural regions. This fragmentation complicates the military landscape in Sudan, as local militias and tribal forces increasingly become involved, further entrenching the cycle of violence. The situation is further complicated by Sudan's historical ties to external actors, including regional powers such as Egypt and the United Arab Emirates, which have vested interests in the outcome of this struggle.

Regional Ramifications of Sudan's Instability

The ramifications of Sudan's internal strife extend well beyond its borders. Neighboring countries, particularly South Sudan and Ethiopia, are already feeling the effects of the unrest. In South Sudan, which shares a porous border with Sudan, renewed violence could disrupt fragile peace agreements and exacerbate existing ethnic tensions. The country has been grappling with its own internal conflicts since its independence in 2011, and a destabilized Sudan could lead to a resurgence of violence and a new influx of refugees.

Ethiopia, too, has a vested interest in the stability of its neighbor. The ongoing Tigray conflict has already strained Ethiopia's resources, and any spillover from Sudan could further complicate the situation. Moreover, the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam (GERD) negotiations, which involve Sudan as a key stakeholder, are at risk of being jeopardized by Sudanese instability. Should the SAF or RSF gain the upper hand, their approach to the dam negotiations could shift dramatically, impacting regional water security and diplomatic relations.

The International Community's Role

The role of the international community in Sudan's crisis cannot be understated. The United Nations and African Union have both expressed concern over the escalating violence and its humanitarian implications. However, their responses have often been hampered by geopolitical complexities and the competing interests of member states. The Sudanese military's historical ties to external actors such as Russia, which has been expanding its influence in the region, complicate the situation further.

Despite the challenges, there are opportunities for diplomacy. The cessation of hostilities and a return to a civilian-led government remain critical for Sudan's future. Engaging regional actors in a coordinated diplomatic effort could provide the necessary pressure on both SAF and RSF to consider a ceasefire and negotiations. However, the historical mistrust between these factions and their respective regional allies poses significant hurdles.

Looking Ahead: A Fragile Tomorrow

The future of Sudan hangs in the balance. As the conflict continues to evolve, the potential for further fragmentation of the military landscape increases, leading to a scenario where various factions operate autonomously, with little regard for national unity. This fragmentation could result in a protracted conflict reminiscent of Somalia's civil war, which lasted for decades and destabilized the entire Horn of Africa.

Moreover, the humanitarian implications are dire. The United Nations estimates that millions are in urgent need of assistance as food shortages and displacement rates soar. The international community must not only respond to the immediate humanitarian crisis but also lay the groundwork for a sustainable peace process that addresses the root causes of the conflict.

As Sudan grapples with its identity and future direction, the coming months will be crucial. The interplay of internal power dynamics and external influences will shape not only Sudan's fate but also the stability of the Horn of Africa. The international community must remain vigilant and proactive, engaging with Sudan's leaders and regional actors to foster an environment conducive to peace and reconciliation. Ultimately, a collaborative approach that prioritizes the interests of the Sudanese people over geopolitical maneuvering will be essential for a stable and prosperous future.
