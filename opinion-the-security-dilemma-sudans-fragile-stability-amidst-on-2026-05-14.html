The Security Dilemma: Sudan's Fragile Stability Amidst Ongoing Conflict

OpinionSudan By Amira Hassan  ·  May 14, 2026
Opinion notice: This is analysis and commentary by Horn Updates editors. It does not represent the position of any government, institution, or external party.
What this means
  • Sudan: humanitarian crisis deepening
  • Regional security risks mounting
  • International diplomatic efforts faltering

Sudan stands at a precipice, grappling with a multifaceted crisis that has escalated dramatically over recent months. The power struggle between rival military factions, particularly the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF), has plunged the nation into a state of chaos. The implications of this turmoil extend far beyond its borders, threatening to destabilize the entire Horn of Africa. This analysis seeks to unpack the current situation in Sudan, examine the regional repercussions, and explore potential pathways forward.

The Roots of Conflict: A Power Struggle

The roots of Sudan’s ongoing conflict can be traced back to the ousting of long-time President Omar al-Bashir in April 2019 following widespread protests against his regime. Initially, hopes were high for a transition to democratic governance. However, the subsequent military takeover in October 2021 thwarted these aspirations and set the stage for a power struggle between the SAF, led by General Abdel Fattah al-Burhan, and the RSF, commanded by General Mohamed Hamdan Dagalo, also known as Hemedti.

In April 2023, tensions boiled over into open conflict, exacerbated by a breakdown in negotiations for a civilian-led government. The violence has since escalated, with both factions engaging in brutal clashes that have resulted in thousands of deaths and the displacement of millions. The humanitarian situation has reached alarming levels, with the UN estimating that over 24 million people—nearly half of Sudan's population—are in need of aid. The conflict has created a dire need for immediate humanitarian assistance, yet access remains severely restricted due to ongoing violence.

Regional Implications: A Broader Crisis Looms

The ongoing conflict in Sudan poses severe risks to regional stability. Neighboring countries, particularly South Sudan, Chad, and the Central African Republic, are already feeling the effects of the unrest. South Sudan, which gained independence from Sudan in 2011, is particularly vulnerable given its history of civil war and ongoing internal strife. The influx of refugees fleeing violence in Sudan has strained South Sudan's already fragile resources, potentially reigniting ethnic tensions.

Moreover, the conflict has the potential to destabilize Chad, where the government is already grappling with its own internal challenges. Chad’s President Mahamat Idriss Déby has been engaged in efforts to broker peace talks between rival factions within Sudan, but the continued violence complicates these diplomatic initiatives. The spillover effects could lead to an increase in arms trafficking and militant activity in the region, further exacerbating security concerns.

International Response: A Diplomatic Quagmire

Despite the urgent need for intervention, the international community's response has been tepid. The African Union has condemned the violence and called for negotiations, yet concrete actions have been limited. The United Nations has been vocal in its condemnation of the human rights violations occurring amidst the conflict, but with the Security Council divided, effective measures have been elusive.

Furthermore, the geopolitical interests of external powers complicate the situation. The United States, China, and Russia have all shown varying degrees of interest in Sudan's stability, often prioritizing their national interests over a unified approach to peace. The lack of a cohesive international strategy risks prolonging the conflict and may lead to further deterioration of the humanitarian situation.

A Path Forward: Navigating the Complex Landscape

As the situation in Sudan continues to evolve, it is crucial for regional actors and the international community to adopt a more proactive stance. This includes not only humanitarian support but also a commitment to facilitate dialogue among the warring factions. A ceasefire, albeit tenuous, could create space for negotiations and the establishment of a transitional government that includes civilian representation.

Additionally, addressing the underlying issues that fueled the conflict—such as economic inequality, ethnic tensions, and historical grievances—will be essential for any sustainable resolution. The role of neighboring countries in playing a mediating role cannot be understated; collaboration among South Sudan, Chad, and the African Union is vital in crafting a solution that addresses both the immediate and long-term challenges.

In conclusion, the situation in Sudan is a reminder of the complexities that define the Horn of Africa. As the conflict continues to unfold, the stakes are high—not just for Sudan, but for the entire region. The path forward requires a concerted effort to engage all relevant stakeholders in a meaningful dialogue, fostering a climate of peace and stability that has long eluded the region.
