Unraveling Justice: The Struggle for Accountability in South Sudan's Courts

OpinionSouth SudanSudan By Amira Hassan  ·  April 17, 2026
Opinion notice: This is analysis and commentary by Horn Updates editors. It does not represent the position of any government, institution, or external party.

The recent trial concerning the brutal killing of Ggaba children in South Sudan underscores a troubling reality: the nation’s legal system is struggling to provide justice for its citizens. The prosecution's inability to present crucial evidence—such as videos, photographs, and a signed visitors book—highlights systemic flaws that threaten the integrity of judicial processes. As South Sudan continues to grapple with its violent past, the question of accountability looms large, revealing deep-seated issues that extend beyond individual cases.

Systemic Failures in Evidence Gathering

The prosecution's struggle in the Ggaba case exemplifies a broader issue within South Sudan's criminal justice system. In many instances, law enforcement lacks the basic tools and protocols necessary for effective evidence collection. The absence of video footage and physical evidence, such as blood spots, raises serious concerns about the investigative capabilities of the police. This is not an isolated incident; similar failures have been observed in a range of cases throughout the country, where victims and their families are left in a state of limbo, awaiting justice that often never comes.

The lack of a structured approach to evidence management can often be traced back to inadequate training and resources for law enforcement personnel. In a nation where the rule of law is still taking root, the absence of a functional judiciary system hampers the pursuit of justice. Such systemic failures not only undermine public confidence in legal institutions but also reinforce a culture of impunity that has long plagued South Sudan.

Political Implications of Judicial Inefficiency

Compounding these challenges is the political environment in South Sudan, where legal proceedings are often influenced by political agendas. The government's focus on stability and maintaining power frequently overshadows its commitment to justice and accountability. Recent statements from U.S. diplomats emphasize the need for the UN peacekeeping mission in South Sudan to adapt its efforts, hinting at a broader recognition that existing frameworks may no longer suffice in addressing the complexities of South Sudan's political landscape.

The interplay between politics and justice cannot be overstated. When political elite are implicated in crimes, or when public sentiment favors political stability over judicial accountability, the judicial system is further compromised. This duality not only obstructs the path to justice but also perpetuates cycles of violence, as victims and their families see their grievances ignored or inadequately addressed.

Women in Business: A Struggle Against Systemic Barriers

As South Sudan grapples with issues of justice, another pressing concern is the plight of women-led businesses in Juba, which are being stifled by high taxes and gender-based violence (GBV). The intersection of economic and gender-based challenges poses significant threats to the empowerment of women in the region. For many women entrepreneurs, the struggle for basic safety and fair taxation is compounded by the broader failures of the legal system, which often fails to protect their rights.

The implications of these systemic barriers are profound. Women, who comprise a vital segment of the entrepreneurial landscape, are being pushed further into the shadows. Data from local organizations indicate that GBV remains a pervasive issue, and high taxation only exacerbates the difficulties faced by women in business. The lack of legal protections creates an environment where women are often left to fend for themselves, further entrenching their economic marginalization.

Looking Ahead: The Necessity of Reform

As South Sudan confronts these pressing challenges—a faltering legal system, political interference in justice, and economic barriers for women—there is a critical need for comprehensive reform. Strengthening the rule of law through training, resources, and community engagement is essential to rebuilding trust in the judicial system. Moreover, addressing the specific barriers facing women entrepreneurs can foster economic resilience and contribute to broader societal stability.

The path forward requires a concerted effort from both the government and civil society to create an environment where justice is not merely an ideal but a tangible reality for all citizens. For South Sudan to emerge from its tumultuous past, a commitment to accountability and equitable economic opportunities must be at the forefront of its national agenda. Without these changes, the specter of violence and injustice will continue to loom over the nation.
