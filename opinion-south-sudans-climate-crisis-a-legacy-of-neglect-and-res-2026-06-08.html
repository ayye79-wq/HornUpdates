South Sudan's Climate Crisis: A Legacy of Neglect and Resilience

OpinionSouth Sudan By Amira Hassan  ·  June 8, 2026
Opinion notice: This is analysis and commentary by Horn Updates editors. It does not represent the position of any government, institution, or external party.
What this means
  • Climate impact worsening in South Sudan
  • Need for sustainable policies grows
  • Local resilience can drive change

As South Sudan struggles to stabilize after decades of conflict, it faces another formidable challenge: the climate crisis. This week, an opinion piece highlighted that South Sudan is paying the price for a climate crisis it did not create. The harsh realities of climate change are compounding the struggles of a nation already grappling with socio-political instability and economic fragility. The world must recognize that the climate crisis is not an abstract phenomenon but a tangible threat that is deeply intertwined with South Sudan's existing vulnerabilities.

Legacy of Neglect: Climate Crisis Meets Conflict

South Sudan's journey toward independence in 2011 was marred by an immediate descent into civil war, which lasted until a fragile peace agreement was signed in 2018. While the country's leaders grapple with political issues, the environmental degradation stemming from climate change has gone largely unaddressed. The 2022 report from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) highlighted that South Sudan is one of the most vulnerable countries to climate change, with rising temperatures and erratic rainfall patterns threatening the agricultural base that sustains its population. According to the Food and Agriculture Organization, nearly 60% of South Sudan's population faces severe food insecurity, a situation exacerbated by climate-related shocks.

The neglect of environmental issues mirrors the broader governance failures that have plagued South Sudan since its inception. Political leaders have primarily focused on resource control and power struggles, sidelining critical discussions about sustainable development and climate resilience. This neglect has led to a fractured relationship between communities and the environment, resulting in a cycle of exploitation and degradation. The country’s rich natural resources, including oil and minerals, have not translated into environmental sustainability or economic stability for its people.

Community Resilience Amidst Crisis

Despite the grim context, South Sudanese communities display remarkable resilience and adaptability in the face of climate challenges. Recent initiatives, such as the cultural ceremony in Torit where the Mura-Hatiha and Tirangore communities pledged peace, illustrate a growing recognition of the importance of unity in addressing shared environmental issues. Local leaders and community members are increasingly aware that sustainable practices can enhance food security and community resilience.

Moreover, grassroots movements are emerging that prioritize environmental stewardship. The local efforts to promote sustainable agricultural techniques, such as agroforestry and crop diversification, reflect a shift in mindset among some communities. These initiatives highlight the potential for self-organization and local agency in confronting the climate crisis. According to a recent report from the United Nations Development Programme, such community-led actions are crucial for building resilience against climate shocks.

The Role of Governance and Policy in Climate Adaptation

For South Sudan to effectively address its climate challenges, significant governance reforms are essential. This week, the South Sudan People's Defense Forces (SSPDF) Chief asserted that the peace agreement has not collapsed, emphasizing the need for stability to foster development. Yet, without a robust framework for environmental governance, any peace achieved will be tenuous at best.

The recent call by the Gender Ministry Undersecretary for market regulations to curb youth substance abuse underscores a broader need for policy coherence. Addressing climate change should be integrated into all sectors, including health, education, and economic development. Policymakers must recognize that climate adaptation and mitigation are not separate from national security and economic stability; they are integral components of a comprehensive strategy for recovery and growth.

International support will also be crucial. The global community must prioritize funding and technical assistance for climate adaptation projects in South Sudan. Sustainable infrastructure investments, such as improved irrigation systems and renewable energy sources, can empower communities to withstand climate shocks. Simultaneously, the international community must hold South Sudan's leaders accountable for implementing effective environmental policies and ensuring that resources are allocated toward climate resilience rather than short-term political gains.

Looking Forward: A Path to Sustainable Development

As South Sudan navigates the dual challenges of climate change and post-conflict recovery, the path forward must prioritize sustainable development. The resilience of its communities is a testament to their ability to adapt, but without concerted efforts from both local leaders and the international community, these efforts may falter under the weight of systemic neglect and inadequate governance. By embracing a future that integrates environmental sustainability into the core of its development strategy, South Sudan can not only safeguard its resources but also foster a more stable and prosperous nation. The time for action is now; the legacy of the past must not dictate the future of this young nation.
