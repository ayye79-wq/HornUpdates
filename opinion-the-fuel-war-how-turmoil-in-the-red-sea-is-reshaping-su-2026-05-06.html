The Fuel War: How Turmoil in the Red Sea is Reshaping Sudan's Conflict

OpinionSudanEthiopiaEritreaSomalia By Amira Hassan  ·  May 6, 2026
Opinion notice: This is analysis and commentary by Horn Updates editors. It does not represent the position of any government, institution, or external party.
What this means
  • Sudan's fuel crisis deepening
  • Regional maritime tensions increasing
  • Humanitarian access severely restricted

The ongoing war in Sudan is not just a local conflict; it is a complex interplay of power struggles that have far-reaching implications. At the heart of this turmoil is a critical yet overlooked factor: the control and distribution of fuel. As battles rage on the ground, a parallel fuel war has emerged, influenced by broader regional dynamics, particularly those involving the Red Sea. The recent disruptions in this vital maritime route are reshaping the conflict landscape in Sudan, creating new challenges for both combatants and civilians alike.

The Role of Fuel in the Sudanese Conflict

Fuel is the lifeblood of any military operation, and in Sudan, its importance is magnified by the ongoing civil war. Since the outbreak of hostilities in April 2023, fuel scarcity has exacerbated the humanitarian crisis, with prices skyrocketing and access becoming increasingly limited. According to reports, the price of fuel has more than tripled since the conflict began, leading to dire consequences for civilians reliant on transportation and basic services.

Combatants are vying for control over fuel depots and supply routes, with the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the paramilitary Rapid Support Forces (RSF) employing aggressive tactics to secure their positions. Recent clashes in strategic areas, such as Port Sudan and Khartoum, underscore the importance of fuel as a military asset. The RSF, in particular, has been accused of seizing fuel supplies from government facilities, further complicating the power dynamics within the conflict.

Disruptions in the Red Sea: A Game Changer

The Red Sea has long been a critical maritime route for global trade and regional security, but recent disruptions have turned this area into a flashpoint. Increased piracy, coupled with geopolitical tensions involving regional powers, has led to a precarious security situation that directly impacts Sudan. For instance, the blockade of the Bab-el-Mandeb Strait, a crucial passage for oil shipments, has heightened tensions and limited access to fuel supplies.

This disruption has dual implications for the conflict in Sudan. Firstly, it hampers the SAF and RSF's ability to sustain their military operations, as fuel shortages limit mobility and operational effectiveness. Secondly, the consequent rise in fuel prices affects ordinary Sudanese citizens, who are already grappling with the consequences of war, including displacement, food scarcity, and lack of medical supplies.

Humanitarian Consequences and International Response

Humanitarian organizations have warned that the fuel crisis could lead to a catastrophic situation for millions of Sudanese. With the ongoing violence preventing aid from reaching those in need, the situation is becoming increasingly dire. In a recent statement, the World Food Programme highlighted that "without fuel, our ability to deliver food and medical supplies is severely compromised." The agency has reported a 70% reduction in its operations due to the escalating conflict and fuel shortages.

The international response has been tepid, with calls for a ceasefire and humanitarian access often overshadowed by geopolitical interests in the region. The U.S. State Department condemned the recent targeting of the Khartoum airport, urging both sides to halt hostilities. However, the lack of concrete action has raised concerns about whether diplomatic efforts can effectively address the complexities of the fuel war and its implications for civilian life.

Looking Ahead: The Need for Comprehensive Solutions

As the conflict in Sudan continues to evolve, it is clear that the control of fuel supplies will play a critical role in shaping the outcome of the war. The current situation calls for a multi-faceted approach that not only addresses the immediate humanitarian crisis but also seeks to stabilize the region. International actors must recognize that the fuel war is not merely a side issue but a central element of the conflict that requires urgent attention.

In conclusion, the ongoing turmoil in Sudan, exacerbated by disruptions in the Red Sea, highlights the intricate links between fuel supply and military power. Without a concerted effort to address these dynamics, the humanitarian situation is likely to worsen, leading to further instability not just in Sudan but across the Horn of Africa. The international community must prioritize negotiations that consider the critical role of fuel in the conflict, ensuring that humanitarian access is restored and that a sustainable peace process can begin.
