The Fragile Peace in Sudan: Will External Influences Derail Hope for Stability?

OpinionSudan By Horn Updates  ·  April 2026
Opinion notice: This is analysis and commentary by Horn Updates editors. It does not represent the position of any government, institution, or external party.

In recent weeks, Sudan has witnessed a spiraling humanitarian crisis, a stark reminder of the fragility of its political landscape. The ongoing conflict between the Sudanese Armed Forces (SAF) and the Rapid Support Forces (RSF) has led to widespread displacement, food insecurity, and a healthcare system on the brink of collapse. Amid this chaos, the potential for peace remains tenuous, particularly as external actors increasingly enter the fray, raising questions about the implications of their involvement.

The Humanitarian Toll

Reports indicate that since the conflict reignited in April 2023, over 1.1 million people have been displaced, with millions more on the brink of famine. The World Food Programme has warned that nearly 6 million people in Sudan face severe food shortages, exacerbated by the ongoing violence and the breakdown of supply chains. This humanitarian crisis is not merely a statistic; it represents the suffering of families, particularly women and children who bear the brunt of conflict. As international organizations scramble to provide aid, their efforts are continually hampered by insecurity and restricted access to affected areas.

External Influences: A Double-Edged Sword

The geopolitical landscape surrounding Sudan is increasingly complicated by the involvement of external powers. Countries such as Russia, the United States, and regional players like Egypt have vested interests in the outcome of the conflict. For instance, Egypt’s continued support for the SAF stems from its strategic concerns regarding the Nile River and its management. Meanwhile, Russia has sought to expand its influence through military partnerships and agreements, including a recent deal to establish a naval base on Sudan's Red Sea coast.

This growing involvement raises critical questions about the motivations behind these external influences. Are they genuinely aimed at stabilizing Sudan, or do they serve narrower national interests? Historical precedents indicate that foreign interventions can often lead to unintended consequences, as seen in Libya and Syria. In Sudan, the potential for external actors to exacerbate divisions rather than promote dialogue is a pressing concern.

The Role of Diplomacy and Regional Alliances

While the situation appears dire, there are avenues for diplomatic engagement that could help stabilize Sudan. The African Union (AU) has been attempting to mediate between the conflicting parties, but its efforts have thus far yielded limited results. The recent Berlin Conference aimed at addressing the crisis in Sudan has generated considerable buzz, but skepticism remains high regarding its efficacy. Many Sudanese view such conferences as disconnected from the realities on the ground, where the voices of ordinary citizens are often drowned out.

Moreover, the success of any peace initiative will depend heavily on regional actors' willingness to cooperate. For example, the Intergovernmental Authority on Development (IGAD), which includes Sudan’s neighbors, has a unique role to play in facilitating dialogue. However, the effectiveness of IGAD has historically been hampered by internal divisions and lack of political will among member states.

Towards a Sustainable Resolution

As Sudan navigates this turbulent period, it is essential for both local and international actors to prioritize a sustainable resolution over short-term gains. Engaging with communities affected by the conflict and incorporating their perspectives into peace negotiations could foster a more inclusive approach. Additionally, ensuring humanitarian access and alleviating immediate suffering should be paramount, as a stable environment is a prerequisite for any meaningful political dialogue.

Looking ahead, the engagement of external powers must be recalibrated to focus on supporting a Sudanese-led peace process rather than pursuing their strategic interests. If done correctly, this could lay the groundwork for a more stable and prosperous Sudan. The international community must recognize that the path to peace is not paved with coercion or self-serving agendas, but through genuine support for the Sudanese people’s aspirations for a peaceful and democratic future.
